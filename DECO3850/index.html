Cubits

Say hello to your new personal assistants.

Cubits has one goal: to get you more focused on your task.

  • Monitor your goals in real time - Popular task management software like Trello and Visual Studio Team Services lack accountability. The Cubits shelf and live feed ensure pressing tasks cannot be ignored.
  • Utilise natural emotional cues - Current task management software have lost potential in appealing to emotion to entice good work. Each cube utilises colour, sound and emotion to send strong messages on team performance.
  • Reinforce a healthy workplace culture - A physical task management device means that your activies are made public. Noticing other team member's tasks encourages active communication and collaboration.

Ethnographic Investigation

I observed potential users in their environment. I took note of how tasks are allocated, distributed and managed.

Participatory Design

Users were involved in designing and choosing the sounds, faces and colours to ensure maximum appeal and motivation.

User Research

I validated the success of Cubits with user observations and surveys.

Iterative Process

The development of Cubits followed a 4-step design life cycle to ensure a timely response to user feedback.

Concept

Cubits was partially inspired by my own experiences working in software development.

My literature reviews found that similar products had lost potential in emotional design, and public goal monitoring.
My user research revealed that active communication between team members were highly valued.
We planned physical cubes representing tasks to enhance goal monitoring, establish order, and persuade employees to good behaviours.

Process

Cubits went through several preliminary designs via an iterative design process.

We devised a system in which cubes will be stacked on a pillar, and pulled out from the bottom one by one.
I helped develop a low-fidelity prototype test for thier functionality and adherence to the user journey.
I developed the Photon software to respond to user interactions, as well as the accompanying live feed.

Evaluation

Cubits were evaluated for their adherence to public goal monitoring and emotional design.

The final product was placed on display at UQ's Interaction Design Exhibit.
Exhibit visitors would interact with the product, and I would take written observations and give out survey questions.
Users reacted generally positively to the product, but flaws in how tasks are percieved and how users collaborate were found.
